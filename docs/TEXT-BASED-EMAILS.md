# Text-Based Email Rules and Examples

Text-based emails are plain-text-style messages that look like they were written by a real person, not designed by a marketing team. They are one of the most underused and highest-performing tools in e-commerce email marketing.

---

## Why Text-Based Emails Work

### 1. Deliverability
Email providers like Gmail use image-to-text ratio as a spam signal. Graphic-heavy emails with minimal text are more likely to land in Promotions or Spam. Text-based emails look like personal correspondence and are more likely to land in the Primary inbox.

### 2. Pattern interruption
99% of e-commerce emails are graphic-heavy. When every email in a subscriber's inbox is a designed template, a plain-text message stands out. It breaks the pattern and earns attention precisely because it looks different.

### 3. Reduced buyer resistance
A graphic email signals "this is marketing." A text-based email signals "this is a person talking to me." That shift in perception lowers the reader's guard and makes them more receptive to the message.

### 4. Higher reply rates
Text-based emails feel conversational. Subscribers are more likely to reply, which creates a direct feedback loop and further improves sender reputation with email providers.

### 5. Fast to produce
No design needed. No Figma modules. No designer queue. A text-based email can be written, reviewed, and sent in under 30 minutes.

---

## Hard Rules

Every text-based email must follow these rules without exception.

### Under 250 words
Hard limit. Text-based emails work because they are short and scannable. Once you cross 250 words, the email starts to feel like a newsletter, and the personal-message effect disappears. If you cannot say it in 250 words, it should be a graphic email.

### 1-2 CTAs maximum
One button or link is ideal. Two is the absolute maximum. The CTA should be visually distinct -- a button that stands out against the plain text. The goal is one action, not a menu of options.

### Named sender
Every text-based email must come from a real person (or a believable persona). The "From" name should be a first name and brand: "[Name] from [Brand]". This reinforces the personal feel. Never send a text-based email from a generic brand name.

### P.S. section required
The P.S. line is the most-read line in any email. It is where you put your strongest hook -- the offer, the deadline, the key CTA, or the one thing you want them to remember. Every text-based email must end with a P.S.

## Example Templates

### Example 1: Promotional / Urgency (Cyber Monday Style)

```
Subject: ENDS TONIGHT

Hey {{ first_name|default:'there' }}!

These are the final hours of our [Sale Name] deals...

[X]% OFF when you spend $[Amount] and [Y]% OFF sitewide. We're running
out of [popular product], which you can get for free when you spend
over $[threshold].

On top of all this -- you can get an EXTRA $[amount] OFF with code: [CODE].

Click here to shop now. By tomorrow, everything goes back to full price.

Save on [product] today, spend more on [aspiration] tomorrow!

Best,
[Name] from [Brand]

P.S. Code [CODE] expires at midnight. This is your last shot.
```

**Word count:** ~100
**CTA:** One inline link + one implied CTA in P.S.
**Best segment:** 90-Day Engaged or 180-Day Engaged (for major sales)

---

### Example 2: Promotional / Supplement Sale

```
Hey {{ first_name|default:'friend' }},

I genuinely believe [product category] changed my life, and I want
the same for you.

Our biggest sale of the season ends tonight, [Date]. Don't wait.

>> [CTA BUTTON: SUBSCRIBE & SAVE 20% OFF]

Your [health goal / benefit] deserves this.

With love,
[Name]

P.S. This is our best price of the year. After tonight, it goes
back to full price. Don't miss it.
```

**Word count:** ~70
**CTA:** One button + one reinforcement in P.S.
**Best segment:** 90-Day Engaged, Win-Back

---

### Example 3: Founder Note with P.S.

```
Hey {{ first_name|default:'there' }},

I've been thinking about something lately. When we started [Brand],
the goal was simple: [1-sentence mission or origin hook].

What surprised me was how many of you wrote in saying [customer
feedback or result]. That's what keeps us going.

If you haven't tried [product] yet, I think you'd love it. It's
[1-sentence value prop or objection answer].

[CTA BUTTON: TRY IT TODAY]

Warm,
[Founder Name] from [Brand]

P.S. Use code [CODE] for [X]% off your first order. Expires [Date].
```

**Word count:** ~110
**CTA:** One button + one code in P.S.
**Best segment:** High Potential Purchasers, 90-Day Engaged

## When to Schedule Text-Based Emails

### Placement in the calendar

- Space them at least 1 week apart. Never send two text-based emails in the same week.
- Best days: Tuesday, Wednesday, or Thursday (mid-week, when inbox attention is highest).
- Avoid sending text-based emails on the same day as a major graphic sale email.

### Monthly rhythm (3x/week cadence)

In a 13-email month:
- **Email 3 or 4** (end of Week 1 or start of Week 2): Educational text-based
- **Email 9 or 10** (end of Week 3 or start of Week 4): Promotional text-based

This places one in the first half and one in the second half of the month, creating two pattern interruptions that keep the calendar fresh.

---

## Monthly Minimum

**1 educational + 1 promotional = 2 text-based emails per month**

This is the floor, not the ceiling. You can send more if the brand voice supports it, but never fewer than 2.

| Type | Pillar | Example Angle |
|---|---|---|
| Educational | Educational | Founder sharing a tip, ingredient story, or behind-the-scenes insight |
| Promotional | Sales | Flash sale reminder, discount code with expiry, last-chance nudge |

For higher-frequency calendars (4x/week = ~17 emails), consider 3 text-based emails: 1 educational, 1 promotional, and 1 community/founder message.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Writing too much
The number one mistake. If the email crosses 250 words, cut it. Ruthlessly. The P.S. line should carry the weight -- the body just sets it up.

### 2. Including images or design elements
A text-based email with a banner image or styled header is not a text-based email. It is a poorly designed graphic email. Keep it pure: text, one button, signature, P.S.

### 3. Using a generic sender name
"[Brand] Team" or just "[Brand]" as the sender defeats the purpose. The whole point is that it feels like a message from a person. Use a name.

### 4. Forgetting the P.S.
The P.S. is not optional. It is the most-read line. Skipping it means leaving the strongest real estate in the email empty.

### 5. Stacking multiple CTAs
"Shop the sale, read the blog, follow us on Instagram, and refer a friend" in one text email destroys focus. One email, one action.

### 6. Not varying the angle
If every text-based email is "hey, we have a sale," subscribers will tune out. Alternate between educational (tips, stories, ingredient deep-dives) and promotional (offers, codes, urgency).

### 7. Sending them too frequently
Text-based emails work because they are rare. If more than 25% of your monthly emails are text-based, the pattern-interruption effect wears off. Stick to 2-3 per month.

### 8. Making the tone too formal
Text-based emails should read like a casual message from a friend or colleague. Avoid corporate language, stiff greetings, or overly polished prose. Write like you talk.
